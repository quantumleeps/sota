What this corpus suggests

⟡ This whole page is my inference — read it as such

Everything below is my reasoning across these 20 papers, not a claim any one of them makes. Citation chips mark what I reason from, not sources that endorse the conclusion. Nothing here is a forecast of where the field is going — it is a reading of a two-month, search-shaped sample of preprints, several of them position papers or single-run reports. Treat it as a structured hypothesis, not a verdict.

A reference model: the orchestration stack

The twenty papers stop looking scattered if you read them as filling in layers of one stack — the set of decisions any LLM multi-agent system has to make, from the wire up to governance. No paper draws this stack; it is my attempt to unify their mechanisms.

⟡ Six layers, and where the corpus works on each

  • 1 · Substrate — the channel. Typed protocols and message buses: the five-axis protocol taxonomy Sander+ '26 and the negotiation exchange Lorenzoni+ '26.
  • 2 · Coordination policy — who acts, in what shape. Learned routing, topology, and role/model selection: AgensFlow Koenigstein '26, Maestro Wu+ '26, InfraMind Kabir+ '26, Orchestra-o1 F. Zhang+ '26, MOSAIC Bao+ '26, MAAD R. Li+ '26.
  • 3 · Boundary content — what crosses, and when. Controlled disclosure of a belief Y. Li+ '26 and step-level streaming Z. Yang+ '26.
  • 4 · Learning signal — where reward and credit attach. The orchestration-trace framing C. Zhang '26, plan-level reward models Tsang+ '26, decision-level RL F. Zhang+ '26, predictive-reward co-evolution Wang+ '26.
  • 5 · Governance — stopping, verifying, monitoring. Sequential stopping Morandi '26, adversarial review + audit cascade R. Yang+ '26, confidence-based screening Keramati+ '26.
  • 0 · Two invariants under all of it. The base model is the ceiling R. Li+ '26, and diversity — not headcount — is the lever that buys independent evidence Bertalanič+ '26.

The lifecycle survey's four-part collaboration taxonomy (Role / Communication / Orchestration / Interaction) maps cleanly onto layers 1–2, and its failure-attribution and self-evolution stages sit at layer 5 — which is why I treat it as the corpus's connective tissue rather than a single data point Qi+ '26.

Bets this corpus supports

⟡ Bet 1 — spend the agent budget on diversity, not headcount

The single most actionable, best-evidenced design principle here: independent evidence comes from de-correlating agents (different model families), not from adding more copies. The scaling law makes this quantitative and falsifiable Bertalanič+ '26; ARIS bets its architecture on cross-family review for the same reason R. Yang+ '26. Corollary: pilot at N≤5 to predict the N=30 ceiling before paying for the team.

⟡ Bet 2 — make less cross the boundary, and structure what does

Raw transcript-passing is a weak coordination medium. Disclose a calibrated belief, not a transcript Y. Li+ '26; forward an early reliable reasoning step, not a finished response Z. Yang+ '26; wrap it in a typed envelope, not free text Lorenzoni+ '26. All three cut tokens and, in two cases, raise accuracy.

⟡ Bet 3 — govern the loop; don't fix the rounds

Stop treating "N rounds of debate" as a constant. Make stopping a decision — a sequential test reclaims 3.7× of the calls and doubles as a failure detector Morandi '26 — and notice the survey flags that nobody yet learns when to stop as an RL target C. Zhang '26. That gap is, to me, the highest-leverage open problem in layer 5.

⟡ Bet 4 — attach the reward to the plan and the decisions, not the final answer

Terminal-only rewards drown in credit diffusion as traces grow. The corpus's efficiency wins all come from moving the signal: up to the plan (Orch-RM, ~10× cheaper) Tsang+ '26 or down to each orchestration decision (DA-GRPO) F. Zhang+ '26. If you must keep it terminal, add a structural format reward to protect the protocol Wu+ '26.

⟡ Bet 5 — treat the orchestration prompt and the topology as security surfaces

Two papers independently turn coordination structure into an attack vector: the orchestration prompt can leak a reward-hacking channel Sun+ '26, and disclosing the communication topology can itself collapse cooperation El Mir+ '26. Design reviews for multi-agent systems should inspect what each role can see, not just what each role can do.

Where I would hold back

◇ Bets I would not place on this corpus alone

  • That debate-to-consensus is a capability multiplier at frontier strength. The corpus's debate evidence is sceptical or near-saturated/underpowered Bertalanič+ '26 Morandi '26; I would treat debate as a budget to govern, not a default win.
  • That cross-family adversarial review is proven superior. Its strongest in-corpus statement is a design analogy backed by a single observational run, with family separation not even enforced in code R. Yang+ '26. Promising, unproven.
  • That single-system SOTA numbers rank approaches. Almost none are significance-tested or cross-team-replicated; read them as existence proofs (see Evidence).
  • That "at-scale" autonomy/negotiation claims transfer to production. The headline scale results lean on synthetic agents or one overnight run Lorenzoni+ '26 R. Yang+ '26.
  • Anything about "where the field is heading." Twenty preprints over two months, shaped by a search query, cannot support a trend line — only a snapshot.

⟡ The one-sentence version

On this corpus's evidence, the frontier of multi-agent work has shifted from "build a smarter agent" to "learn a better coordinator" — and the same papers that chase that shift also show coordination is capped by the base model, fragile to a single bad actor, and not yet measurable by any shared benchmark. The interesting bet is not whether multi-agent orchestration helps, but whether learning the coordination outruns the ceiling on coordination itself. This corpus opens that question; it does not close it.
