From prompts to context engineering

Before "codified context" can be a design discipline, the corpus has to agree on what context is and why engineering it is different from writing a clever prompt. Three definitional moves recur: context as an assembled set of typed components, context as an operating system for the agent, and prompts/specs as first-class software artifacts.

The naming of a discipline

The most cited move is to declare context engineering a discipline that supersedes prompt engineering. The 1,400-paper survey defines it as "a formal discipline that transcends simple prompt design to encompass the systematic optimization of information payloads for LLMs" Mei+ '25 Definition, and frames the shift as one of register:

It shifts the focus from the "art" of prompt design to the "science" of information logistics and system optimization.— A Survey of Context Engineering for LLMs Mei+ '25, §Comparison of Paradigms

The code-generation survey says the same thing in operational terms — context engineering is "not merely an extension of prompt design, but rather a dynamic methodology whose core objective is to deliver the right information and tools to the model — at the right time and in the appropriate format" Dong+ '25 — and adds the consequential claim that "many failures of intelligent agents do not stem from the models themselves, but rather from defects in the ingested context" Proposal. The practical paper that coined the multi-tool framing defines it as "systematically constructing and supplying all relevant information needed for a coding task… via a coordinated multi-agent process" Haseeb '25.

Context as an assembled set of components

The survey gives the formalism the rest of the field implicitly uses. Where prompt engineering treats the context as a single static string, context engineering treats it as a dynamically assembled set produced by a high-level function C = A(c₁, c₂, … cₙ), whose components map to distinct informational roles Mei+ '25 Definition. That decomposition is the cleanest lattice onto which the codified artifacts of this thread fall.

The survey also separates foundational Components (retrieval & generation, processing, management) from System Implementations (RAG, memory systems, tool-integrated reasoning, multi-agent systems) Mei+ '25 — a useful reminder that "codified context" is a Component concern that gets realised through those Implementations. It flags a structural asymmetry worth keeping in mind: models "demonstrate remarkable proficiency in understanding complex contexts" but "exhibit pronounced limitations in generating equally sophisticated, long-form outputs" Theory — i.e. feeding rich context is easier than getting rich output back.

Context as the agent's operating system

The corporate-scale paper pushes the metaphor furthest: context is "an active execution environment, not a passive buffer for prompts" — it "manages memory, allocates resources, isolates processes, provides a unified interface," i.e. it behaves like an OS for the agent Vishnyakova '26 Theory. It names three pressures any context manager must trade against — a relevance deficit, a memory deficit (finite window), and a budget deficit (every token costs) — and proposes five production-grade quality criteria for context:

Relevance

the minimum sufficient for the decision, not "everything available."

Sufficiency

enough to act without guessing.

Isolation

processes/contexts kept from contaminating each other.

Economy

token cost is a first-class constraint.

Provenance

where each piece of context came from.

note Vishnyakova '26

The paper states the last two "are not explicitly distinguished" in the Google/LangChain sources it builds on — i.e. it claims them as its own contribution.

Good context is not "everything available" but "the minimum sufficient for the decision."— From Prompts to Corporate Multi-Agent Architecture Vishnyakova '26, §What Constitutes Good Context

The maturity Pyramid

Its central scaffold is a cumulative four-level model where each discipline subsumes the one below as load-bearing foundation, not as a replacement Vishnyakova '26 Proposal:

LevelOptimisesHorizonScale
Prompt engineeringquery formulationone callindividual
Context engineeringthe information environment (the "OS")hours–daysteam / product
Intent engineeringgoals, values, trade-off hierarchiesweeks–monthsbusiness unit
Specification engineeringmachine-readable corpus of policy/standardscontinuouscorporation / ecosystem

The paper's slogan ties them together: "whoever controls the agent's context controls its behaviour; whoever controls its intent controls its strategy; whoever controls its specifications controls its scale," and frames specifications as "the constitution of a nation of agents" Vishnyakova '26. It also draws a distinction the multi-agent pages lean on — delegation ≠ decomposition: decomposition splits a task into parts, delegation transfers authority and decision rights; conflate them and a multi-agent system "degenerates into a distributed monolith."

◇ The author's own caveat

This is explicitly a "conceptual framework." The paper concedes the central "emergence" hypothesis "has no formal proof yet… engineering intuition backed by practice rather than a mathematical theorem," that machine-readable specs scale poorly from PDF/verbal policy, and that measuring context quality "remains experimental territory" Vishnyakova '26.

Prompts and specs as software artifacts

The third foundational move treats the artifacts themselves as software, subject to the full engineering lifecycle. "Promptware engineering" names a "promptware crisis": prompts are first-class software artifacts but built by "ad hoc… trial-and-error" Chen+ '25 Definition. It supplies a C1–C10 taxonomy of what makes natural-language prompts resist traditional tooling (non-determinism, fuzzy correctness, capability boundaries, memory management) and a roadmap of 27 research opportunities across requirements → design → implementation → testing → evolution → deployment — including prompt versioning/traceability, pattern repositories, and even "prompt compilation." It is explicitly unvalidated and vision-stage.

The "Agentic SE" pillars paper makes the artifact turn concrete for coding: it calls for replacing "transient, informal prompts" with "durable machine-readable structured artifacts that serve as contracts and institutional memory" Hassan+ '25 Proposal, and proposes named, version-controlled artifacts — BriefingScript (the work order, an "evolution of literate programming"), LoopScript (declarative workflow/SOP), and MentorScript ("mentorship-as-code") — explicitly naming CLAUDE.md/AGENTS.md as the grassroots precursor to a codified MentorScript. It also notes the gap that motivates this whole thread:

[There is] no mainstream tool… [providing] adequate observability, archival, or unified revision control over the combined code + prompts + conversational context… [CLI agents leave context] only in a terminal's scroll-back buffer.— Agentic Software Engineering: Foundational Pillars Hassan+ '25, §Discussion

Two kinds of knowledge to codify

The Siemens framework gives the cleanest answer to what goes into codified context, by interviewing experts and finding their knowledge splits in two: explicit procedural rules (clear if-then logic, directly translatable into executable code/tools) and tacit design principles (contextual judgement that "couldn't be reduced to simple rules" and must enter through prompts/RAG) Ulan uulu+ '26 Definition. Crucially, neither alone sufficed — "code lacked analytical insight while LLM-only solutions produced domain-inappropriate outputs."

⟡ Why this two-type split matters downstream

I read the explicit/tacit split as the theoretical justification for why a codified-context layer needs both executable artifacts (skills, scripts, MCP tools ≈ explicit rules) and prose guidance (CLAUDE.md/AGENTS.md ≈ tacit principles) — and why "just write better docs" or "just add more tools" each fail on their own. The other papers' empirical finding that context files are heavy on commands/architecture but light on the "why" (see Codified Context) looks, through this lens, like an over-investment in explicit rules and an under-investment in tacit principles.

Where this leaves the foundations

The corpus has converged on a shared premise — context is engineerable, and the engineering is a software discipline — without yet converging on a shared schema. The component formalism Mei+ '25, the OS/maturity framing Vishnyakova '26, and the artifact-lifecycle framing Chen+ '25 Hassan+ '25 are three vocabularies for the same object. The next page looks at what that object actually looks like once developers start writing it down.
