The measured-effect ledger

What was actually quantified across these 20 papers, with method, scale, and a confidence appraisal. The corpus mixes strongly-powered studies (a scaling law fit to 44 conditions, a 27-model benchmark) with single-run system reports and synthetic-agent simulations — so the final column, my confidence, is where the reading earns its keep.

⟡ My confidence scale — not from the papers

The right-hand column is my appraisal of how much weight each result bears, not a label the authors assigned. Strong = well-powered, replicated across conditions, or significance-tested with clear margins. Moderate = a real, sizable effect on genuine benchmarks but from a single system/setup, no significance test, or with a known confound. Indicative = small-N, single-run, synthetic, exploratory, or flagged underpowered by the authors themselves. Numbers are the papers' own primary results unless noted as cited.

Appraisal — where the evidence is consistent, and where it is thin

⟡ My reading of the ledger

Two claims are consistently supported across independent setups in this corpus: (1) learned/structured coordination beats static wiring or naive scaling — Maestro, InfraMind, StreamMA, Orch-RM, AgensFlow, Orchestra-o1 all show it on real benchmarks, several with ablations isolating the coordination component; and (2) naive scaling of agents/communication has sharply diminishing returns — the scaling law (strong), DarkForest, and Sequential Consensus all point the same way. The evidence is thinner exactly where the field's excitement is highest: long-horizon autonomy rests on a single observational run R. Yang+ '26; "at-scale" negotiation uses synthetic agents Lorenzoni+ '26; and almost no system reports significance tests or cross-team replication. Treat single-system SOTA numbers as existence proofs, not rankings.

◇ What would raise confidence (drawn from the papers' own future work)

The corpus names its own missing instrument: a MAS-native benchmark that jointly reports task success, parallelism efficiency, collaboration quality, and protocol overhead at realistic trace lengths — none exists in the pooled literature C. Zhang '26. Beyond that: live-LLM (not synthetic) negotiation at scale Lorenzoni+ '26; controlled, compute-matched comparisons of single- vs cross-family review R. Yang+ '26; scaling-law tests on role-asymmetric and verifier-aggregator pipelines (the paper offers explicit falsification conditions) Bertalanič+ '26; and standardized orchestration datasets for reward modeling Tsang+ '26.
