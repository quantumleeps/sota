State of the Art · Multi-Agent Systems & Orchestration

The orchestrator becomes the model

Across these 20 papers — all arXiv preprints from May–June 2026 — the recurring move is to treat coordination itself as the object of engineering. As individual LLMs plateau on bounded tasks, the leverage shifts to how work is decomposed, delegated, communicated, aggregated, and stopped across a team of agents. The papers converge less on "which model is smartest" and more on a system question: which orchestration policy turns a pool of models, tools, and skills into a reliable whole — and when does adding agents stop helping?

It is a reading of this corpus — a search-shaped, two-month sample of mostly un-peer-reviewed preprints, several of them systems descriptions or single-run reports — not a complete survey and not a claim about where the field is heading. What recurs across these 20 papers

Claims with enough independent support within this corpus to count as a shared view here — not field-level verdicts.

1 · Orchestration is being recast from fixed wiring into a learned, adaptive policy Proposal Empirical

The strongest throughline: stop hand-designing pipelines and learn the coordination decisions. AgensFlow treats coordination as "an online policy-learning problem under partial observability" Koenigstein '26; Maestro trains a 4B policy to route over model-skill ensembles Wu+ '26; InfraMind learns planner/executor/scheduler jointly as one MDP Kabir+ '26; Orchestra-o1 trains the main agent's decisions with DA-GRPO F. Zhang+ '26; and the RL survey reframes the whole problem around the learnable orchestration trace C. Zhang '26. → Learning to orchestrate

2 · More agents and more talk is not more intelligence Empirical

Several papers independently find diminishing — sometimes negative — returns to scale and chatter. Effective team size hits a hard ceiling (thirty debaters ≈ one) Bertalanič+ '26; exposing more raw inter-agent text can hurt accuracy, so disclosing only a compact belief beats sharing transcripts Y. Li+ '26; fixed-round debate wastes calls a sequential test can reclaim 3.7× Morandi '26; and when a reasoning step arrives matters more than how much context does Z. Yang+ '26. → Debate & scale

3 · The base model sets a ceiling; orchestration amplifies, it does not rescue Theory Empirical

Coordination magnifies a capable model and exposes a weak one. MAAD names orchestration a "capability amplifier" bounded by the base model's competence, "a performance ceiling… not a bottleneck coordination can bypass" R. Li+ '26; the scaling law shows stronger/thinking models reach the ceiling with fewer agents Bertalanič+ '26; and coding-strong models are not orchestration-strong Sun+ '26. → Frameworks

4 · Reward and credit should attach to the orchestration plan, not just the final answer Proposal Empirical

The RL-for-MAS work converges on supervising coordination directly. The survey organises rewards into eight families with an MAS-specific "orchestration reward" and an eight-level credit hierarchy, noting events — not agents — carry credit C. Zhang '26; Orch-RM scores the plan before sub-agents run, 10× cheaper Tsang+ '26; DA-GRPO rewards step-level orchestration decisions F. Zhang+ '26; Maestro shows outcome-only RL is the primary driver Wu+ '26. → Learning to orchestrate

5 · Communication needs structure and standards, not free-form transcripts Definition Proposal

The communication layer is being formalised. A five-axis taxonomy classifies nine protocols (MCP, A2A, ANP, ACP…) and predicts a federated, layered stack Sander+ '26; LLM-X builds a typed-envelope negotiation bus Lorenzoni+ '26; DarkForest replaces transcript-passing with controlled disclosure of a calibrated belief Y. Li+ '26; StreamMA refines the transmission unit to the reasoning step Z. Yang+ '26. → Communication & coordination

6 · Coordination is fragile and adversarially exploitable, and evaluation lags Empirical Theory

A sober counter-current. A single deceiver collapses group cooperation, and merely disclosing the topology can be an attack surface El Mir+ '26; long-horizon agents fail silently via "plausible unsupported success" R. Yang+ '26; the survey catalogs failure-attribution as an open problem Qi+ '26; and no pooled benchmark jointly measures task success, parallelism, collaboration, and protocol overhead C. Zhang '26. → Limitations
