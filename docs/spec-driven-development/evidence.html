What was actually measured

Most of this corpus is proposal, taxonomy, and position — only five of the 14 papers report original quantitative results. This ledger collects those measured effects, with method and scale, so a controlled number is never confused with a vision statement. The "my confidence" column is my appraisal, not the papers'.

⟡ My confidence scale — mine, not the papers'

Strong = multiple settings, real artifacts, independent validation, sizeable effect. Moderate = a real measurement but single-model / single-domain / proxy-metric / LLM-judged. Indicative = small sample, small effect, mixed signal, or a metric that may not mean what it appears to. Every row below is a paper's own result; figures the papers merely cite from other work are listed separately at the bottom and kept out of this ledger.

Effect directions are coloured by the papers' own framing (more valid specs / fewer security failures = good; a 44.4% ceiling is reported as a deficit). MAAD's structural metrics carry the authors' own "paradox of complexity metrics" caveat — low complexity from a weaker model can mean under-specification, not quality.

⟡ Appraisal — where the evidence is consistent, and where it is thin

Consistent: every paper that put a checkable artifact in front of an automated oracle showed the spec/gate adding signal — Suresh's fuzzer caught unit-test survivors, Grynets' explicit security spec lowered hidden-test failures monotonically, Taghavi's grounding held test compatibility while nudging quality. The direction "an explicit, machine-checkable spec helps" is the corpus's best-supported empirical claim.

Thin: effects are often small (Spec Kit +0.15 quality, +1.7 SWE-bench), single-model and single-domain (Grynets one API + one model; Taghavi one generator), proxy- or LLM-judge-mediated, and rarely significance-tested. The clearest human signal in the corpus — Taghavi's blinded preference test — did not favour the more elaborate pipeline. And the upstream capability the whole paradigm leans on, specification-level reasoning, sits at 44.4%. Read the ledger as "promising and directionally consistent," not "settled."

◇ What would raise confidence (from the papers' own future work)

Multi-model and multi-domain replication (Grynets, Taghavi, Li); human-validated gold labels and human baselines for SpecBench (Hamblin); process-level benchmarks for whole SDD frameworks rather than per-tool docs (de Macedo); controlled evaluation of TDD-governance vs baseline prompting (Hasanli, currently preliminary); and longitudinal team studies of agentic development's effect on technical debt and maintenance (Bhati, De La Cruz, Mandl).
