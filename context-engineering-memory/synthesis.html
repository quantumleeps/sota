A reference model and some bets

⟡ This entire page is my inference — not the papers'

Everything below is my reasoning across the corpus: a model that unifies the findings, and the design bets I'd make on top of them. Citation chips here mark what I'm reasoning from, not sources that endorse the conclusion. No single paper proposes this stack or these bets; treat them as one reviewer's view, not consensus.

The reference model: one managed context stack

The cleanest way I can fit these sixteen sources together is to stop seeing "context engineering" and "agent memory" as separate fields and see one tiered store whose fastest, scarcest level is the context window. Each paper turns out to operate at a specific tier, and the live debates are about how to move information between them.

Five bets I'd make

Bet 1 · Cache, not vault

Treat the window as a cache with an explicit, cost-aware eviction policy, and default to aggressive forgetting. The inverted cost model — keeping is expensive, re-fetching cheap — makes deletion the right reflex, against the "remember everything" instinct. Mason '26

Bet 2 · Curate before you compress

Prefer lossless moves (clear re-fetchable payloads, branch, retrieve just-in-time) and let the model distill detail itself; reach for lossy summarization only when the bloat is reasoning you cannot reconstruct. Zhang+ '25 (ACE) Anthropic '26 (Cookbook)

Bet 3 · Learn the policy

Memory management should be learned and query-aware, not a fixed schedule. The 2026 systems consistently show learned, on-demand control beating hand-coded heuristics on both quality and cost. Yu+ '26 (AgeMem) Zhang+ '26 (BudgetMem)

Bet 4 · Co-design memory with the agent

Don't bolt a store onto a fixed agent. MemoryArena's representation/training mismatch says the store and the policy that consumes it must be shaped together, or the gains evaporate. He+ '26 (MemoryArena)

Bet 5 · Measure cost and decisions, not recall

Evaluate the end-to-end pipeline on decision quality and token cost, and test forgetting. Recall on a saturated QA set is a vanity metric. Du '26 Hu+ '25 OpenAI '26

The throughline

Across all five: information logistics is becoming a first-class part of the agent, not a wrapper around it. The window is the cache, the store is the disk, and the policy that pages between them is increasingly the model's own job.

Where I'd hold back

◇ Bets I would not place yet

  • Fully RL-driven memory in production. AgeMem/MemSkill/BudgetMem are promising, but they're single-team, single-backbone, no-significance results; the training cost and reward-design fragility are real. Compelling direction, premature default.
  • Parametric / weight-baked memory. Hard to audit, update, or delete — and unlearning is not production-ready. I'd keep memory inspectable and external until that changes Du '26.
  • "Architecture X wins." The corpus can't support a winner: too few cross-team comparisons, benchmarks shaped by their authors, and one adversarial benchmark that humbles all of them He+ '26 (MemoryArena).
  • Over-trusting the OS analogy. Demand paging is a productive metaphor, but its own inverted-cost finding shows where the analogy breaks; borrow the abstractions, not the conclusions.
