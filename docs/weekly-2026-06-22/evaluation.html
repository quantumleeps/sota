03 · Evaluation & Serving

Measuring and running agents honestly

The last three papers ask how we know an agent is good and how we run it at scale. One argues the leaderboard number we report doesn't predict deployment and proposes a different criterion; one builds a harder benchmark where strong subtask scores evaporate over a full workflow; one treats the agentic workload's own runtime variability as a first-class systems problem. The first two extend this week's skeptical register to evaluation itself; the third is the infrastructure underneath all of it.

Rank by predictive validity, not by the mean

Patel+ '26 is a position paper with a blunt thesis: aggregate-score leaderboards "systematically underspecify the dimensions on which deployed LLM agents are evaluated." Its proposed replacement criterion is predictive validity — the correlation between an in-sample ranking and an out-of-sample one — measured over a twelve-tier apparatus that spans tool-call hygiene, planning quality, cost, failure modes, multi-turn dialog, and evidence-grounded verification, most of which no single prior benchmark reports.

▦ The ranking doesn't transfer Empirical

In a 149-team agentic competition, the correlation between public and hidden rankings was ρ = −0.13 on execution (n=13, p=0.71 — indistinguishable from zero) and 0.69 on planning, whose upper confidence bound just touches the paper's proposed falsification threshold of 0.85 Patel+ '26. A separate cross-benchmark study it cites found rank correlations of just 0.32–0.85 across six benchmarks. Empirical

The deployment-relevant question is not "what is the mean," but "does this ranking transfer." — Beyond Static Leaderboards Patel+ '26, §2.3

◇ Weigh this as convergence, not replication Theory

The paper is explicit that it "do[es] not run new controlled experiments"; its evidence is 14 parallel implementation studies that converged on the same measurement dimensions from different architectural starting points — "convergent architectural sensitivity," not independent triangulation, since the studies share institutional context (an IBM benchmark + Columbia course projects) and are unpublished. It is also entirely in one domain (industrial asset operations). I read its diagnosis (aggregate scores hide deployment behaviour) as well-supported by the ρ=−0.13 datapoint, and its prescription (predictive validity + twelve tiers) as a reasonable but as-yet-unvalidated proposal — which is how the paper frames it too.

When subtask competence doesn't survive the workflow

Zou+ '26 (LabOSBench) drags computer-use evaluation into a harder regime — controlling scientific instruments, where success depends on feedback-driven parameter adjustment, not just clicking the right widget. It's browser-native (Playwright, no OS virtualization), 96 subtasks across eight instrument simulators, and it can grade intermediate scientific state (e.g. PSNR on a focus task), not only final completion.

▦ A 0.814 agent that finishes 26% of workflows Empirical

The best method (GTA1 w/ GPT-5.5) reaches a 0.814 average subtask score (human 0.924) — but in the end-to-end pilot, average workflow success is only 26.3%, "much lower than its subtask-level performance, indicating severe error accumulation" Zou+ '26. Several subtasks (precise alignment, focus, tilt, mosaic-ROI save) score 0.00 for all twelve methods. And an agent wrapper can be the worst method overall (VLAA-GUI w/ Opus-4.5, 0.456). Empirical

simply adding an agent loop is not enough: wrong clicks, incorrect control adjustments, and invalid recovery actions can still derail scientific procedures — LabOSBench Zou+ '26, §Category-level analysis

This is the evaluation-side mirror of the cognition findings: the gap between a benchmark's headline subtask number and what an operator would see end-to-end is exactly the gap Patel+ '26 says aggregate leaderboards conceal — here made concrete as a 0.814-vs-0.263 cliff, and as the long-horizon ceiling the Menaged+ '26 automata work measures from the cognition side.

Serving an agent is its own scheduling problem

Underneath evaluation sits execution. Huang+ '26 (SwarmX) observes that an agentic workflow's inference time and call structure depend on prompt semantics — revealed only at runtime — so conventional cluster schedulers misfire. Its answer is "agentic scheduling": routers and scalers become lightweight scheduler agents that use compact, distribution-aware neural predictors and keep predictions distributional until the final decision, because for low-QPS agentic workloads a single bad decision dominates the tail.

SwarmX is the one paper in this sample that is unabashedly a building result rather than a skeptical one — a production-deployed system (128 H20 GPUs in test; 320 H20 + 560 L20 in production; CPU clusters over a million cores) with strong tail-latency wins. Its honesty markers are systems-paper-typical: several ablations omitted "due to space," eventual-consistency drift in predictor weights, and the semantic model needing retraining on a target-model change. Notably it, too, frames a classic systems component — the cluster scheduler — as an "agent," extending the week's appetite for casting infrastructure as learned policy.

⟡ My inference — not from the papers

Stack these three and you get a full vertical slice of "agent quality": SwarmX runs the workload, LabOSBench measures whether the run actually finished the job, and Patel+ argues the number we publish about that should be a transferable rank, not a mean. None cites the others, and I'm imposing the stack — but it's a useful way to see that two of the three (eval, measurement) belong to this week's audit-your-defaults mood, while the third (serving) is the unsexy infrastructure that has to work regardless of mood.
