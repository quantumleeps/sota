A reference model: the spec-driven loop

The papers describe different pieces of one recurring shape. Read together, they sketch a closed loop in which the human holds the two ends and an agent runs the middle:

▦ The loop these papers, taken together, describe

  1. Intent (conversation). Exploratory dialogue discovers what is wanted — but is not yet executable Koch '26.
  2. Contract (reviewed, grounded spec). Intent is frozen into a structured, human-reviewed artifact, validated against repository reality before any code Taghavi+ '26 Marron '26.
  3. Generation (agent). An agent (or agent team) produces code/architecture/tests from the contract Li+ '26.
  4. Automated check (oracle). A mechanical check tests output against the spec — fuzzing, hidden suites, a deterministic engine, schema/phase gates Suresh+ '26 Grynets+ '26 Hasanli+ '26.
  5. Evidence-gated acceptance (risk-tiered). Output is admitted on evidence proportional to risk, not on plausibility; humans sign off at the gates that matter Koch '26 Monperrus '26.
  6. Governance, cross-cutting. Identities, permissions, sandboxing, logging, provenance, rollback wrap the whole loop De La Cruz '26 Mandl+ '26.

The human is at step 1 (intent) and step 5 (accountable gate), and the governance wrapper; the agent owns 2–4. This is my unification of Bhati's A-SDLC, Koch's SCOPE-V, Mandl's harness, and the verification cluster — none of them states it in exactly these terms.

Bets this corpus supports

⟡ Bet 1 — the durable artifact is the spec plus its oracle, not the prose

The empirical wins all came from a spec something could mechanically check: a fuzzer Suresh+ '26, a hidden security suite Grynets+ '26, repository test gates Taghavi+ '26. I'd bet the lasting unit of "spec-driven development" is the spec bound to an automated check — a prose spec with no oracle behaves, on this corpus's evidence, more like a prompt.

⟡ Bet 2 — "conversation-to-contract" discipline will matter more than prompt quality

The convergence from "isolated prompt" to "reviewed brief / autonomous session over the repo" recurs independently Koch '26 de Macedo '26. I'd bet teams get more from a gate that forces a reviewed contract before execution than from further prompt-tuning.

⟡ Bet 3 — risk-tiered acceptance beats both uniform review and uniform automation

Koch's R0–R3 gates and Monperrus's "humans at the high-stakes margins" point the same way: match scrutiny to risk rather than reviewing everything or trusting everything Koch '26 Monperrus '26. Grynets is the reminder that even the top tier needs a hidden oracle Grynets+ '26.

⟡ Bet 4 — process maturity differentiates teams — but only above a base-model floor

de Macedo and Mandl locate the leverage in process structure, not tool choice de Macedo '26 Mandl+ '26; Li's "performance ceiling" says orchestration can't rescue a weak base model Li+ '26. My read: above a capability threshold, the differentiator is the harness and the gates; below it, no amount of process helps.

⟡ Bet 5 — security and governance have to be specified, not assumed

Grynets shows implicit security underperforms an explicit security spec Grynets+ '26; Marron builds permissions/sandboxing into the spec language Marron '26; De La Cruz names governance debt as the default failure De La Cruz '26. I'd bet the spec-driven SDLC that survives contact with production is the one that treats permissions, provenance, and security as first-class spec artifacts.

◇ Where I'd hold back — bets this corpus does NOT yet support

  • "The end of code review." A compelling argument Monperrus '26, but a position paper, and its own correlated-reviewer and prompt-injection caveats are unresolved. I would not retire human review on this evidence.
  • Full A-SDLC autonomy / "intent-centric platforms." These sit at the least-evidenced end of the corpus's own maturity ladders De La Cruz '26 Bhati '26; the direction is plausible, the timeline is not established.
  • That spec-driven development measurably reduces defects/debt end-to-end. Effects measured are small and local; there are no longitudinal or whole-process results, and the one human-preference test was unfavourable Taghavi+ '26.
  • That agents can be trusted to author the spec. Specification-level reasoning at 44.4% says the upstream step is far from solved Hamblin+ '26; treat AI-drafted specs as drafts Mandl+ '26.

⟡ The one-line takeaway

On the strength of these 14 papers, spec-driven development is best understood not as "write specs then generate code" but as closing a loop: turn intent into a machine-checkable contract, let agents run against it, and accept output on evidence proportional to risk — with humans owning intent and accountability at the ends. The mechanism is coherent and the early, narrow measurements point the right way; the end-to-end payoff, the upstream "get the spec right" problem, and the long-term maintenance story remain, in this corpus, open.
