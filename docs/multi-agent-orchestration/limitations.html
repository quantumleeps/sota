06 · Limitations & Open Problems

What this corpus says is still broken

These are the failure families the papers themselves name — synthesised into groups, with each claim tied to the source that raised it. Read alongside the Evidence appraisal: the open problems below are why even the strong results should be read as existence proofs rather than settled practice.

1 · Coordination is fragile and adversarially exploitable

⚠ A single bad actor — or even a disclosed topology — can collapse a team

One deterministic deceiver drives group cooperation from 77% to 0%, and disclosing a restricted communication topology collapses cooperation that the identical silent restriction preserves — failure by meta-reasoning, not information loss El Mir+ '26. The survey independently catalogs structural MAS threats — inter-agent message pollution, shared-memory contagion, orchestrator misdirection — and finds steerability is not formalised as a training objective anywhere in its pool C. Zhang '26.

⚠ Silent failure: claims that outrun their evidence

For long-horizon autonomy the dangerous failure is invisible — "plausible unsupported success," where results are real but misreported and claims outrun the evidence that licenses them R. Yang+ '26. The orchestration prompt is itself an attack surface: leaking private tests to a coder sub-agent is "a reward-hacking channel created by the orchestration prompt itself" Sun+ '26.

2 · Adding agents and rounds has a hard ceiling

◇ Diminishing — sometimes negative — returns to scale

For homogeneous instruct-class teams, effective team size saturates (thirty debaters ≈ one), peer count and rounds collapse to their product, and the "debate" gain is largely re-evaluation that a noise placebo reproduces Bertalanič+ '26. Sharing more inter-agent text can lower accuracy Y. Li+ '26, and at frontier strength extra debate rounds buy no measurable accuracy Morandi '26. The survey echoes that "more agents ≠ better performance" is an unresolved scalability bottleneck Qi+ '26.

◇ The base model caps the system

Orchestration "elevates… stronger LLMs while exposing the deficiencies of weaker LLMs," setting "a performance ceiling… not a bottleneck coordination can bypass" R. Li+ '26. No framework in the corpus demonstrates that orchestration rescues a weak backbone — every reported win is on a strong one.

3 · The communication layer lacks safety and standards

⚠ Privacy, compliance, and policy enforcement are "widely absent"

Across all nine protocols in the taxonomy, privacy safeguards, compliance checks, and policy enforcement are missing, and decentralized discovery is rare Sander+ '26. The field also has no settled standard: short-term convergence pressure exists, but the authors expect a federated, layered stack rather than one interoperable protocol — leaving today's systems wired to a moving target. Classical distributed-systems defenses (verified channels, reputation, commitment devices) are named but untested in the LLM setting El Mir+ '26.

4 · Credit assignment and evaluation are unsolved

◇ Credit diffuses; stopping is never learned

Under a shared terminal reward the per-unit signal "becomes increasingly indistinguishable from baseline noise as trace length grows," and the counterfactual effect of a spawn decision is not identifiable from on-policy traces alone; explicit counterfactual message-level credit appears in just one pooled entry, and no method trains the stopping decision as an RL target C. Zhang '26. Failure attribution — which agent, which step caused a failure — is itself an open problem with unsettled definitions and synthetic-only data Qi+ '26.

◇ No MAS-native benchmark exists

No open benchmark in the pooled literature jointly measures task success, parallelism efficiency, collaboration quality, and protocol overhead at realistic trace lengths; most report task success only C. Zhang '26. Orch-RM likewise flags the absence of standardized orchestration datasets/benchmarks Tsang+ '26, and several debate/consensus results sit on near-saturated benchmarks where differences are statistically insignificant Morandi '26.

5 · The evidence base itself is thin in places

◇ Single runs, synthetic agents, narrow scope

Concrete reproducibility limits the corpus admits: ARIS's deployment evidence is one observational overnight run ("we do not generalize from it") R. Yang+ '26; LLM-X's "at-scale negotiation" uses synthetic Python agents, not live LLMs Lorenzoni+ '26; the RL survey is single-author-tagged with no inter-annotator agreement C. Zhang '26; the scaling law is bounded to homogeneous, role-symmetric, fully-connected teams Bertalanič+ '26; the confidence-proxy study covers two essay sets and one model family Keramati+ '26; and MAAD evaluates against a single, "somewhat outdated" baseline on subjective metrics R. Li+ '26.

⟡ The cross-cutting tension I see

The corpus is pulled between two of its own findings. It is racing to make orchestration more capable — learned policies, omnimodal teams, infrastructure-aware scheduling — while simultaneously proving that orchestration is more capped and more fragile than the hype assumes: a hard scaling ceiling, a base-model bound, adversarial brittleness, and no instrument to measure collaboration as such. The unresolved question underneath all twenty papers is whether the gains from better coordination (real, repeatedly demonstrated) outrun the limits of coordination itself (also real, repeatedly demonstrated). On this corpus's evidence that is genuinely open — which is the honest place for a two-month snapshot of a fast field to land. My reading; see Synthesis for where I would and would not place bets.
