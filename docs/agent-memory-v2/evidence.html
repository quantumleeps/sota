⟡ My confidence scale — and a blanket caveat

I grade each row Strong / Moderate / Indicative. The blanket caveat first: none of these 14 papers report statistical significance tests, almost all are evaluated by their own authors — frequently on a benchmark the same paper introduces, and often with an LLM as judge — and all are un-peer-reviewed preprints with no independent replication. So nothing here earns Strong. Moderate = a clear, consistent effect across multiple backbones or benchmarks with a controlled ablation. Indicative = a single or self-introduced benchmark, small sample, or pure self-evaluation. Read the column as "how much I'd bet," not "how true it is."

⟡ My appraisal — where the evidence is consistent vs thin

Most consistent: the negative result. That memory validity/adjudication is unsolved (STALE), that storage ≠ usefulness (WorldMemArena), and that append-only accumulation fails — these converge from independent papers using different methods, which is the strongest signal in the corpus. Reasonably supported: efficiency wins (TokenPilot, GenericAgent, JAMEL) and that learned/process-supervised memory beats heuristics (MemTrain, TaskMem), because they show clear ablations and, for cost, near-objective measurement. Thin: every "my system beats baselines" headline — almost all are author-run on self-introduced benchmarks with LLM judges, no significance, and at least two (EvoMem, Bayesian-Agent) document their own method hurting in some settings. The accuracy leaderboard numbers are the least trustworthy part of this corpus; the diagnostic findings are the most.

◇ What would raise confidence (from the papers' own future-work)

Shared cross-system benchmarks not authored by the method's proposers; statistical significance / repeated trials (authors of MUSE and Bayesian-Agent flag wide intervals themselves); replication on real (not synthetic) long-horizon traces (PerMem); dynamic, streaming evaluation rather than static snapshots (FluxMem, WorldMemArena); and schema-free conflict resolution in the wild rather than controlled single-conflict pairs (STALE).
