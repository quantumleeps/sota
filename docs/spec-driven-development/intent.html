04 · The Intent-Centric Turn

When code is abundant, judgment and accountability become the work

The corpus's two reflective papers — plus threads in several others — argue that spec-driven development is a symptom of a larger reframe: software engineering is moving from authoring code to authoring intent and owning accountability. As generation gets cheap, the durable human contributions become specifying the problem, calibrating trust in agent output, and governing autonomy. This page is heavier on theory than measurement — both anchor papers are interpretive, not experimental.

Code-centric → intent-centric

De La Cruz's reflexive thematic analysis names the shift and its central tension Definition Theory:

GenAI makes creation cheaper, while agentic software engineering makes accountability more important. — From Code-Centric to Intent-Centric Software Engineering De La Cruz '26, §Findings

"Intent-centric" engineering means humans express outcomes, constraints, quality attributes, policies, and acceptance evidence while agents generate, test, repair, and explain. The paper's destination is not autonomy but bounded autonomy Definition:

The trajectory is not autonomy without humans. It is bounded autonomy inside engineered control loops. — De La Cruz '26, §Introduction

It also names a new liability the spec-driven SDLC creates — governance and provenance debt Definition: the deficit that accrues when agents gain tool/repo access without identities, permissions, sandboxing, review gates, audit logs, provenance, and rollback. Governance, the paper insists, "cannot be an afterthought." The analysis is organised as eight themes and a five-stage maturity pathway (individual assistance → governed team use → lifecycle-integrated workflows → intent-centric platforms → bounded-autonomous engineering), and a matrix of six "acceleration vs accountability" dichotomies (e.g. augmentation vs automation, prompting vs architecture, autonomy vs governance) De La Cruz '26.

◇ What kind of evidence this is

De La Cruz is a qualitative discourse analysis of public + scholarly sources (talks, essays, posts, papers), not an interview or repository study. Its per-theme "source support" counts (e.g. 25 artifacts for the convergence theme) "indicate traceability breadth," not statistical weight, and the maturity pathway is "an analytic projection rather than a predictive model" De La Cruz '26.

The competencies that gain value

Alenezi's interpretive synthesis reaches a compatible conclusion about the workforce Theory Proposal:

as code becomes abundant, the durable value of the software engineer increasingly resides in intent specification, critical judgment, and accountable oversight rather than in the sheer volume of code produced. — Human-AI Collaboration and the Transformation of SE Work Alenezi '26, §Abstract

The paper frames three coexisting paradigms (Traditional / GenAI-enabled / Agentic, on an SE 1.0→3.0 ladder) and contributes a Future Software Engineering Competency Framework (FSE-CF). Its competency matrix inverts the traditional coding-centric hierarchy: the competencies with the lowest AI-substitutability and highest strategic criticality are cognitive and governance — critical judgment, systems thinking, trust calibration, and autonomy/accountability policy — with "intent specification" the top cognitive competency that "converts ambiguity into testable acceptance criteria" Alenezi '26. Engineers, it argues, need a "barbell" profile: deep foundations plus AI-collaboration skills, with the routine middle delegated.

▦ Trust is produced by the loop, not assumed of the generator Theory

Alenezi grounds the reframe in cited adoption data (an AIDev dataset of 456,535 agent-authored PRs across 61,453 repos; one developer producing 164 agent PRs in three days vs 176 human PRs over three years) but argues capability has outrun trust: "the binding constraint is shifting from how fast code can be produced to how reliably it can be trusted, integrated, and maintained," and "trust is produced by the loop, not assumed of the generator" Alenezi '26. (Those PR figures are cited from other studies, not Alenezi's own measurement.)

The human relocates to the edges of the loop

This reframe shows up across the corpus as a consistent picture of where the human goes. Bhati: "the developer's role shifts from producing to orchestrating, reviewing, and directing" Bhati '26. Monperrus, having argued away per-commit review, relocates scrutiny upstream and downstream — developers become "specifiers and orchestrators" whose craft "shifts from line-by-line inspection to system-level judgment" Monperrus '26. Mandl keeps humans on approval for "merges, deployments, schema changes, permission changes" Mandl+ '26. The throughline: humans own intent at the top of the loop and accountability at the gates, not the keystrokes in between.

When autonomy is delegated, accountability does not disappear — it concentrates. — Alenezi '26, §Theoretical Implications

⟡ My inference — "intent-centric" is the demand-side story for spec-driven development

The Spec-First and Verification pages describe the supply side — the artifacts and gates that make spec-driven development work mechanically. This page is the demand side: why anyone bothers. If code is cheap and trust is scarce, the spec is where humans exert leverage and where accountability attaches. That reframe is, in this corpus, argued rather than measured — both anchor papers are interpretive, and "intent-centric platforms / bounded-autonomous engineering" sit at the far, least-evidenced end of their own maturity ladders. I find the direction coherent and the timing unproven. Chips mark what I reason from.
