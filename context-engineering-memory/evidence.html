What's actually been measured

A ledger of the corpus's primary measured effects — each paper's own results, not numbers it quotes from others. The final column is my appraisal of how much weight each result can bear, and it is mine, not the papers'.

⟡ My confidence scale — not from the papers

The "confidence" column rates how robustly each effect is established, on my own three-point scale: Strong = multiple datasets and baselines, ideally with replication or significance testing; Moderate = solid single-team empirical work with baselines but limited scope (one model family / one benchmark / no significance tests); Indicative = single-user, observational, or not-yet-validated-at-scale — suggestive, not conclusive. Almost everything below is Moderate or Indicative: this is a young, fast-moving, largely single-team literature.

◇ Secondary-cited figures — kept out of the ledger

The surveys quote striking numbers from other works that are not their own evidence, so they don't belong above. Used carefully and attributed, they include: a 15.3× drop in Voyager's skill-acquisition speed without its skill library, Generative Agents degrading within 48 simulated hours without reflection, GAIA scores of 92% (human) vs 15% (GPT-4), and a reported ~30% accuracy degradation on LongMemEval over long interactions — all cited by Du '26 / Mei+ '25 from primary sources outside this corpus.

Appraisal

⟡ My reading of the evidence — not from the papers

Most consistent: the cost story. Independently, Mem0, ACE, MemSkill, BudgetMem, SideQuest, Demand Paging and CMV all show that curating the window cuts tokens/latency substantially while holding quality roughly flat. That direction is robust because so many different teams and levels reach it.

Weaker: the quality-improvement story. The "+X% accuracy" claims are almost all single-team, single-model-family, no-significance-test results on benchmarks the authors helped shape. And the one adversarial, decision-focused benchmark — MemoryArena — finds that bolting memory onto an agent gives no reliable accuracy gain at all. So "memory makes agents smarter" is, on this corpus, much thinner than "memory makes agents cheaper."

Thinnest: the memory-hierarchy systems (Demand Paging, CMV) rest on single-user corpora and token-estimation heuristics — genuinely novel framings with genuinely preliminary evidence.

◇ What would raise confidence (from the papers' own future work)

  • Multi-team, cross-backbone replication and significance testing — almost none exists today.
  • A standardized harness that reports cost alongside accuracy and tests forgetting, called for by both surveys Du '26 Hu+ '25.
  • Co-training memory representation with the agent, per MemoryArena's diagnosis of representation/training mismatch He+ '26 (MemoryArena).
  • Multi-user, comparative corpora for the systems papers (vs Cursor/Copilot/others) Mason '26 Santoni '26 (CMV).
